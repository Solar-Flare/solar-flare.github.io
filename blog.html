Planck Length

Date: 02/25/2019

There exists a minimum distance we can possible measure: The Planck Length. It is about 1.616 * 10^-35 meters in length. But why is it that we can't observe, say, 1.4 * 10^-35 meters?

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle is an answer to this concept. It states that several properties of a particle has innate uncertainties tying them together. One such pair is location and momentum. The principle states that the uncertainty in location times the uncertainty in momentum is equal to or greater than the Planck Constant.

Suppose you tried to measure a particle's location so accurately down to the planck length and beyond. This makes the uncertainty in location extremely small. Consequently, since the equation must hold, the uncertainty in momentum becomes so large. Since this means its velocity, and consequently its energy, also has a high uncertainty, this results in such a massive energy 'burst' that the particle that is trying to be observed at the planck length level to immediately collapse into a miniature black hole, unable to observe beyond this fundamental unit of length.

In other words, if you want to ruin somebody's day, just observe their shirt down to the 1.616 * 10^-35 meter level.

Binomial Tests

Date: 11/18/2018

Sometimes, you get data that you are skeptical of its validity. Like, if you were running an experiment and you found out that 30% of people who receive coke receive alcohol poisoning, while 100% of people who receive water receive alcohol poisoning.
There's actually a lot of statistical techniques to solve that problem! One of them is the binomial test.

In any experiment, a hypothetical 'average' that the data would reflect on would usually exist. You expect A to be around 8%. You expect those who drink water to receive alcohol poisoning 2% of the time. Using the total sample and total population, you can calculate whether the results of the experiment are valid or not!

A binomial test inputs the probability, sample size and sample's result to determine the probability of the result occurring. In the above example, say we fed 200 people coke, and 60 of them received alcohol poisoning. Also hypothetically assume that coke has a 1% chance of giving someone alcohol poisoning (psst it's actually 100%)
This means that the mean is 2 people and standard deviation is 1.4 people. 60 people, from the experiment, means that the experiment's results is over 40 standard deviations from the mean! It's probability of occurring hence is infinitessimally small (less than 1 * 10^-10% actually). Subsequently, it is unreliable and thus the experiment's results can be thrown out the window.

Normally if the result's probability of occurring is at least 5%, then it is significant enough for the experiment to be considered valid. But otherwise, like the example above, means that rethinking the experiment's procedure and removal of the poisoned bodies is necessary.

Wave Function 2

Date: 11/14/2018

One wave strand should not cause an interference pattern!

Unless...

One wave strand acts like all wave strands!
For as long as a photon is not observed, the pattern emitted will be an interference one, due to the wave function. The wave function is a function that determines where a particle is located. So if a photon was for sure going to be at (0, 0), then it's wave function will equal 1 at (0,0) and 0 at everywhere else.
But if a photon can be at (0,0) with a 75% probability and (0,1) with a 25% probability, then its wave function will match accordingly. As a photon entering the interference board can be at any point, these probability strands are the ones that interfere and thus a interference pattern will occur, albeit hyper paradoxically.

This requires zero observation, however, as once the photon is observed, the wave function collapses immediately and the photon receives a definite location (can be (0,0) or (0,1) with the given probabilities, but only present at 1 point than at both points). Thus, observing it will remove the interference pattern and you will get a single moving point.

65,537 and the Interesting Number Paradox

Date: 09/11/2018

65,537 is a pretty interesting number. It is currently the largest Fermat prime number known, which is a prime number that also happens to be a Fermat number which is a number expressed in the form 2^(2^n) + 1. If n is 4, then 2^4 is 16, and 2^16 is 65536, and plus one is 65,537. No other fermat numbers are currently known to be prime, which makes this number interesting.

The largest n-gon constructible by a compass and ruler where n is odd also turns out to be 65537.

But the concept of 'interesting' depends wildly on perspective. Some may say 65,537 is not a very interesting number and there are more interesting ones (Like 1729). So there may exist varying degrees of 'interesting'.

But, by definition ALL numbers must be interesting.
If 2 were the smallest unintersting number, that itself would make it interesting. Similarly, if there existed a set of uninteresting numbers, the first number of that list would become interesting by virtue of being the first uninteresting number. In other words, a set of uninteresting numbers cannot exist.

In other words we should appreciate mathematics and every number that comes out of it because by sheer definition all numbers are interesting by default. This is the Interesting Number Paradox.

Why is the space black? There is no air to 'reflect' the wavelengths of the light source back into your eyes, so there is no light. Black is the abscence of light, so that is why space is black.

Probabilities Part 2 - Cards

Date: 08/30/2018

A deck of cards have 52 cards. If you were to shuffle them and place them on a pile, they are probably a deck of cards in a order that is never before seen in the entire universe.
Of course, this sounds insane. There are only 52 cards in a deck and there are multiple millions of people that play cards and have shuffled the deck many times in their life. So how can this be?

What do you think the total number of ways to order a deck of 52 cards are? 1 million? 10 million? Maybe a billion or even a trillion?
Mathematically, the total number of ways to order a deck of cards is 52 factorial. This is 8.06 times 10 to the 67... Or 80,658,175,170,943,878,571,660,636,856,403,766,975,289,505,440,883,277,824,000,000,000,000.

In comparison, ever since the big bang, the universe had a total of a mere 4.35 times 10 to the 17 seconds! A deck being a new composition of 52 cards thus is (total number of existing compositions) / 8.06 * 10^67, which is still probably well over 99%.

Even if there are an astronomically large number of something (in this case all known shuffles of the deck in the entire universe), if there is an even astronomically larger number (all possible shuffles), it doesn't matter if the number is still incomprehensibly large, it is completely dwarfed by an even large number and is reduced to insignificance.

Precision and Accuracy

Date: 08/29/2018

What is precision and accuracy in a scientific context?
For instance, lets say that a research study, labelled A, finds that there is 60.5% silicon in gummy bears. Another research study, labelled B, finds that there is 39.99999999% silicon in gummy bears.

Precision is a measure of how many significant digits there are in a number. Simply put, each non-zero digit is a significant digit and all zeros at the end of a number to the right of the decimal point. 39.99999999% would be more precise than 60.5% due to its large array of significant digits.

As we all know the true value of silicon in gummy bears is 100%, we can evaluate the accuracy of the two research studies. That is, how close the result values are to the real value. In this scenario, A is more accurate than B because 60.5 is closer to 100 than 39.99999999.

And lastly, sorry for all gummy bear enthusiasts and haters, but the two research studies are completely, totally false.

Probabilities Part 1 - Coin

Date: 08/26/2018

We normally attribute flipping a coin as having a 50/50 chance, so such is often used to decide one thing out of a pair.
There is one problem: it's not.

What if it lands on its side? Such an event albeit being very rare is still certainly possible. If that happens, do you just not choose both since one side correlates to an option? Or do you just flip again?
It probably happens too rarely for you to even consider that case though, if you were to decide a case by coin flip. No one would stop and think about what to do if it lands on its side, they just flip the coin having defined heads = A and tails = B, without considering what to do if side comes up.

Also, even if you removed the side case, it's still not an even 50/50. Studies have shown that the initial face up side when flipped has a 51% chance of landing at the end compared to the measely 49% the initial face down side has.
Even worse, the coin is deterministic. If you could correctly calibrate the amount of force the coin receives, the amount of drag in the air, collision elasticity, and other factors, it will always land on the same side.

Which brings the question, is life deterministic or free will? Determinism is the idea that everything in life is already planned out, as in, whenever you flip a coin, its variables are all generated by some omnipresent force to give exactly this response. Free will, on the other hand, argues that everything is truly random. The coin flip will truly give a 51/49 response every time and it is not decided by some magical force.
People have debated on this issue for a long time, but the horrifying or fun truth is, we will never know.

But the true question is, how did all of this come from the act of flipping a coin? Human imagination is certainly weird. And creative.
