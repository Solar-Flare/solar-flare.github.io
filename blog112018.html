Binomial Tests

Date: 11/18/2018

Sometimes, you get data that you are skeptical of its validity. Like, if you were running an experiment and you found out that 30% of people who receive coke receive alcohol poisoning, while 100% of people who receive water receive alcohol poisoning.
There's actually a lot of statistical techniques to solve that problem! One of them is the binomial test.

In any experiment, a hypothetical 'average' that the data would reflect on would usually exist. You expect A to be around 8%. You expect those who drink water to receive alcohol poisoning 2% of the time. Using the total sample and total population, you can calculate whether the results of the experiment are valid or not!

A binomial test inputs the probability, sample size and sample's result to determine the probability of the result occurring. In the above example, say we fed 200 people coke, and 60 of them received alcohol poisoning. Also hypothetically assume that coke has a 1% chance of giving someone alcohol poisoning (psst it's actually 100%)
This means that the mean is 2 people and standard deviation is 1.4 people. 60 people, from the experiment, means that the experiment's results is over 40 standard deviations from the mean! It's probability of occurring hence is infinitessimally small (less than 1 * 10^-10% actually). Subsequently, it is unreliable and thus the experiment's results can be thrown out the window.

Normally if the result's probability of occurring is at least 5%, then it is significant enough for the experiment to be considered valid. But otherwise, like the example above, means that rethinking the experiment's procedure and removal of the poisoned bodies is necessary.

Wave Function 2

Date: 11/14/2018

One wave strand should not cause an interference pattern!

Unless...

One wave strand acts like all wave strands!
For as long as a photon is not observed, the pattern emitted will be an interference one, due to the wave function. The wave function is a function that determines where a particle is located. So if a photon was for sure going to be at (0, 0), then it's wave function will equal 1 at (0,0) and 0 at everywhere else.
But if a photon can be at (0,0) with a 75% probability and (0,1) with a 25% probability, then its wave function will match accordingly. As a photon entering the interference board can be at any point, these probability strands are the ones that interfere and thus a interference pattern will occur, albeit hyper paradoxically.

This requires zero observation, however, as once the photon is observed, the wave function collapses immediately and the photon receives a definite location (can be (0,0) or (0,1) with the given probabilities, but only present at 1 point than at both points). Thus, observing it will remove the interference pattern and you will get a single moving point.

Infrared

Date: 11/6/2018

All wavelengths of the electromagnetic spectrum that are not part of the visible section are, frankly, not visible. Since they're not visible, how do we know they exist?
An experiment in trying to figure out the heat of different wavelengths did that. Before, all that was known was that white light would disperse into the different colors due to diffraction when given a narrow slit. An experimenter used a couple of thermometers to measure the temperatures at points struck by red light, yellow light, .etc. and had a few 'control' thermometers not being hit by the colors.

What ended up happening instead was that the thermometer next to the red light thermometer read an abnormally higher temperature!

This when experimented upon led to the discovery of infrared and ultraviolet waves, where 'infra' means below and 'ultra' means above, so infrared is below red in the EM spectrum and ultraviolet is above violet in the spectrum.
